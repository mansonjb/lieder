Rivedoux-Plage is the first village you reach after crossing the bridge from La Rochelle, and its restaurant scene punches well above its size. Seafood dominates, with oysters starring on nearly every menu — not surprising given the oyster beds are literally visible from several terraces. The atmosphere is relaxed and summery, with most tables at their liveliest from May to September.

## Our picks

**Les Viviers en Ré** (Seafood, €€) — The most theatrical address in the village: oysters are brought out alive from the tank in front of you, clams with chorizo are a crowd favourite, and the monkfish holds its own. The terrace looks directly over the channel and oyster beds, which turns even a simple lunch into something worth remembering. The oysters here are known for being plump and fairly priced.

**Restaurant Le M** (Gastronomic, €€€) — The benchmark table in Rivedoux, set inside the Hotel de la Marée by the port. Chef Florent Chagnolaud produces intensely flavoured, ocean-driven cooking: long oysters, hake cooked on the plancha, dishes that convince even those who think they don't like fish. Noted by the Gault & Millau. The brasserie menu at €26.50 is excellent value for a first visit — you get a proper sense of the kitchen without the full bill.

**Restaurant du Grand Large** (Seafood and Charentais, €€) — Facing the southern beach, this is straightforward, honest cooking built around fresh, seasonal, locally sourced ingredients. The kitchen nods to Charentais traditions with occasional flavours from further afield, well judged and never overdone. Ideal for a relaxed lunch with the beach in view.

**L'Embarcadère** (Bistrot, €€) — A seasonal address at Sablanceaux, open spring through autumn, banking on its large ocean-facing terrace. Fish tartare, oysters, mussels, house burgers and grilled meats cover the full range of appetites. Arrive early in high season — the terrace fills fast.

**Le Ré-galet** (Seafood, €€) — Unhurried, unfussy, and satisfying. A well-put-together starter buffet, good fish and shellfish without any pretension, and a sunny terrace. The right address to decompress after a long cycle loop around the island.

**L'Ane qui Tangue** (Bistrot and tapas bar, €€) — The format shifts with the hour: traditional French cooking at lunch, a broad menu of hot and cold sharing tapas in the evening. There is a pétanque court, a sheltered terrace, and parking. In the evening with a group, the tapas format is the most convivial option in the village.

## Rivedoux market

The covered market hall sits on Place de la République in the heart of the village. In July and August it opens every day from 08:00 to 13:00. During spring and autumn it runs on Wednesdays, Fridays and weekends at the same hours. Vegetables, fish, cheeses, charcuterie, artisan bread and regional specialities — it makes a natural stop before heading to the beach for a picnic.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are any restaurants open outside the summer season in Rivedoux?**
A handful of places stay open at weekends in autumn. From November to March, weekday options shrink to one or two addresses — Restaurant Le M being the most reliable. Always check opening hours before making the trip.

**Can I eat oysters directly from a producer?**
Les Viviers en Ré offers exactly that experience in a restaurant setting, with the oyster beds visible from the terrace. If you want to buy direct from the producer to take away, La Flotte and Ars-en-Ré have more oyster shacks to choose from.

**Which table is best for a proper dinner?**
Restaurant Le M at the port is the local reference, with cooking recognised by the Gault & Millau. If you want a wider choice, Saint-Martin-de-Ré is about 12 kilometres away (35 to 40 minutes by bike) and concentrates the finest tables on the island.
