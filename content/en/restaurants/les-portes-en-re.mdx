Les Portes-en-Ré keeps things simple, and that is precisely the point. The northernmost village on the island has only around fifteen places to eat, almost all of them built around the sea and the oyster beds of the Fier d'Ars. You do not come here for a grand gastronomic evening -- you come to eat oysters with sand between your toes, to savour a mouclade under a wisteria-draped terrace, or to pick up the morning market before cycling out towards the dunes.

## Our picks

**Le Bout du Monde** (seafood, €€) -- 9 ter Rue de la Prée, in the heart of the village. The house mouclade -- mussels cooked with curry and Pineau des Charentes -- is one of the best on the island, and the tuna tartare and tapas-style seafood starters are well worth ordering. The wisteria terrace is popular with regulars and books up fast. Open April to November, closed Tuesday and Wednesday. Book ahead in July and August: this is not optional.

**La Cabane de la Patache** (fish and seafood, €€) -- Route de la Patache, facing the Fier d'Ars near the Trousse-Chemise beach. Oysters, original cold mussels, fresh catch of the day. Relaxed atmosphere, practically on the sand. Closes in October and operates in season only -- arrive early or risk having no table.

**Les Huîtres de Trousse-Chemise** (oyster shack, on-site tasting, €) -- Route de la Levée Verte, past the golf course towards the north-western tip of the island. Franck Moreau grows his oysters on site and serves them in a wooden basket with bread, butter, lemon and mignonette. Everything is built from natural materials and the view stretches out over the marshes. No bookings -- order at the bar and wait on the outdoor benches. Open Tuesday to Sunday, 12:00-14:30 and 18:30-21:00. The best value in the village, by some distance.

**La Bazenne** (bistrot, €€) -- on the central village square. A large terrace overlooking the square makes this a natural choice for a relaxed lunch. The menu is straightforward and generous: fresh fish, langoustines, galettes, homemade burgers. A solid family option with no fuss and a good view of the market coming and going.

**Le Bistrot des Portes** (French bistrot, market cuisine, €€) -- 21 avenue du Haut des Treilles. The most valuable address in the village: open all year round, which is genuinely unusual in Les Portes-en-Ré. Home cooking with local produce, a set lunch menu for under 20 euros, and a warm atmosphere in a room of wood and natural stone. Rated 9.1/10 -- deservedly so. Out of season, this is your only real option on site, and it is a very good one.

## The Les Portes-en-Ré market

On the central square, from 8:00 to 13:00. In high season, every day. In low season (except January), on Tuesdays, Saturdays and Sundays. Around thirty stallholders: market gardeners, oyster sellers, charcuterie, cheeses, island sea salt, flowers and local crafts. This is one of the liveliest markets in the north of the island. Arrive before 9:00 if you are after the best oysters.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do you need to book in Les Portes-en-Ré?**
In July and August, yes -- without question. The choice of restaurants is small and demand is high. Le Bout du Monde in particular often fills up by midday. In June and September you can try your luck, but calling the day before is a sensible precaution.

**Are there restaurants open out of season in Les Portes-en-Ré?**
Only one operates all year: Le Bistrot des Portes. The others close between October and March or April. Out of season, that is your only option on site -- or a cycle to Ars-en-Ré, about 8 km away.

**Can you eat oysters directly in Les Portes-en-Ré?**
Yes, and it is one of the best reasons to visit. Les Huîtres de Trousse-Chemise offer tastings straight from the oyster farm, facing the marshes. Unbeatable freshness at very reasonable prices.

**Is Les Portes-en-Ré suitable for a family meal out?**
Yes, particularly La Bazenne on the square and La Cabane de la Patache by the beach. Avoid arriving without a plan in August: tables go quickly and alternatives are limited.
