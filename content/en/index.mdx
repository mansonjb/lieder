There are islands you visit. Île de Ré is an island you return to.

Eighty-five square kilometres of Atlantic light, ten communes that seem designed to be lived slowly. Saint-Martin-de-Ré and its UNESCO-listed Vauban ramparts. La Flotte with its blue-shuttered houses pressing tight around a fishing harbour. Ars-en-Ré, whose black-and-white spire once guided sailors home. Loix, a peninsula within a peninsula, known mainly to those who already know it.

## Why the British love Île de Ré

The island has been a favourite with British visitors for decades, and for good reasons. The crossing by Brittany Ferries overnight from Portsmouth to La Rochelle is comfortable, the island is 20 minutes from the port, and the journey from the UK takes no longer than many domestic drives. You arrive with your car, your bikes, your dog, your luggage: nothing has to be managed.

Once there, the appeal is clear. Over 100 kilometres of cycle paths on flat terrain, warm and calm sea on the eastern coast, excellent seafood, good wine, and a pace of life that slows you down within a day. The island works well for families, for cycling holidays and for anyone who wants France without the motorway.

## Two coasts, two characters

To the east, the sheltered pertuis charentais: calm, shallow water that warms quickly in summer. Families, kayakers, morning swimmers. To the west, the Atlantic: open, breezy, with real waves, surfers and those long July sunsets that stretch past ten in the evening. Between the two: a few kilometres. On the same island, you can swim in flat water in the morning and watch kitesurfers in the afternoon.
