The salt marshes of the Île de Ré are one of its defining landscapes — flat, luminous, full of birds and alive with an ancient industry still practised today.

## The landscape

The marshes cover the northern and central parts of the island, a mosaic of water channels, clay-lined pans and drying areas. The light here is extraordinary — wide sky, reflective water, the kind of flat luminosity that painters and photographers seek out.

## The salt harvest

Paludiers (salt workers) harvest sea salt from July to September, using techniques unchanged for centuries. Fleur de sel — the fragile crust that forms on the surface — is harvested by hand with a flat wooden implement and is among the finest salt in France.

## The Ecomuseum of Salt Marshes (Ars-en-Ré)

The island's main resource for understanding salt production. Interactive displays, guided tours of the marshes, tasting. Open in season; check hours.

## Birdwatching

The marshes attract egrets, herons, avocets, black-winged stilts and many other species. The Lilleau des Niges reserve, adjacent to the marshes, is one of France's most important migratory bird sites.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can you buy fleur de sel directly from the paludiers?**
Yes — several salt workers sell directly from their cabanes. The market at Ars-en-Ré also has local salt vendors.

**When is the best time to see the salt harvest?**
July to September. Active harvesting depends on weather conditions — hot, dry, windy days produce the best fleur de sel.
