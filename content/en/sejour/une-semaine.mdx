A week on the Île de Ré is the island's natural unit. It is long enough to cover the whole island at a relaxed pace, find your own preferred corners and still have time to do nothing in particular. Most British visitors who arrive by ferry stay for a week or two, and a week leaves almost nothing you wished you had done.

## Arriving by ferry

If you are coming from Portsmouth on the overnight Brittany Ferries crossing, you will arrive in La Rochelle on Sunday morning. The island is 20 minutes from the port. For villa and house rentals, changeover day is almost always Saturday, so arriving Sunday puts you a day ahead rather than fighting for access. Consider booking La Rochelle accommodation for Saturday night if this is the case.

If you arrive by TGV, Sunday morning trains from Paris arrive in La Rochelle around noon.

## How to structure the week

**Day 1 (arrival)**: Settle in, buy food, explore your village on foot. Pick up hire bikes if needed. Swim or walk before dinner.

**Day 2 (eastern villages)**: Cycle or drive Rivedoux, Sainte-Marie-de-Ré, La Flotte. La Flotte market on weekday mornings. The harbour at La Flotte deserves an hour.

**Day 3 (Saint-Martin-de-Ré)**: Walk the Vauban ramparts, explore the citadel, lunch at the port. The town is most pleasant in the morning before coach parties arrive. Market on Wednesdays and Saturdays.

**Day 4 (marshes and Ars-en-Ré)**: Cycle through the salt marshes. Ars-en-Ré for lunch. The church tower and the salt market. Loix in the afternoon if you want silence.

**Day 5 (western tip)**: Drive or cycle to the lighthouse at Saint-Clément-des-Baleines. Walk Les Portes-en-Ré. The Atlantic coast around here is the wildest and least crowded part of the island.

**Day 6 (beach day)**: Pick your beach and stay there. Les Grenettes for families. Les Gollandieres or La Conche des Baleines for space and waves. Pack a picnic.

**Day 7 (favourite places again)**: Return to wherever worked best. Buy salt, jam, oysters for the journey home. Leave time to sit in a café at the port with nothing scheduled.

## What a week covers

Every village. Both coastlines. The lighthouse. The nature reserve at Lilleau des Niges. The salt marshes. The best markets. The best restaurants. The island at a pace that actually feels slow.

## Accommodation strategy

Base in one place. Moving accommodation mid-week wastes time and energy. For a family of four, a house or villa in the central part of the island (Le Bois-Plage, La Couarde, Sainte-Marie) puts you within cycling or driving distance of everything.

If you want village life and restaurants on foot, Saint-Martin is the obvious choice. If you want space, quieter beaches and more room, anywhere on the western half suits better.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a week long enough to do everything?**
Yes. A week is the ideal duration for a first visit. Longer trips are wonderful but only if you genuinely want to slow down rather than see more.

**Should you move accommodation during a week?**
Most visitors base in one place. The island is small enough (30 km end to end) that you can reach anywhere in under an hour by car or bike.

**Is a week too long if you have young children?**
No. The beach, the bike paths and the slower pace suit young children very well. A week goes quickly with children; two weeks is manageable.

**What is the best time of year for a week's stay?**
Late June, early July or September. Warm enough to swim, less crowded than the August peak, and restaurants less stretched. August works but book everything well in advance.
